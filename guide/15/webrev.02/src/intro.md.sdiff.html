 7 When the JDK was released under the GPLv2 license in May 2007, the need for a
 8 detailed document for contributors was anticipated. Translating rather
 9 sophisticated institutional knowledge into a format suitable for non-Oracle
10 employees is always a challenge. This document attempts to address the needs of
11 developers who wish to participate in the future of the JDK.

 7 Welcome to the OpenJDK Developers' Guide!




 8 
 9 OpenJDK is the place to collaborate on an open-source implementation
10 of the Java Platform, Standard Edition, and related projects.
11 OpenJDK was created when the JDK was first released under the GPLv2 license in May
12 2007 and since then a community has grown around it to help develop and maintain Java.
13 
14 In order to work together efficiently clear directions are sometimes needed to
15 avoid misconceptions and to align developers' views of terminology and process.
16 The OpenJDK community is a fairly pragmatic place. "Do what's right" is most
17 often the right course of action. Still, if people do things in the same right way
18 everyone's work becomes more transparent and easier for others to follow. For
19 this reason most parts of the development process have standard flows that are the
20 recommended ways to do things.
21 
22 The goal of this guide is to answer questions that the developers of the JDK might
23 have around development process, tooling, standards, etc. The formal rules and
24 processes are described in other documents, like [JEP 1](https://openjdk.java.net/jeps/1)
25 for the JDK Enhancement-Proposal & Roadmap Process, or [JEP 3](https://openjdk.java.net/jeps/3)
26 for the JDK Release Process. This guide is meant to be a complement with tutorials
27 and examples for how to follow these rules and how to work together with the rest
28 of the OpenJDK community.
29 
30 There are many common use cases that aren't detailed in the formal process. This
31 guide contains the defacto standard for how to work in such cases.
