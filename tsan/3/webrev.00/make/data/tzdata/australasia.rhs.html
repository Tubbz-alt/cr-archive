2100 # The International Date Line is not defined by any international standard,
2101 # convention, or treaty.  Mapmakers are free to draw it as they please.
2102 # Reputable mapmakers will simply ensure that every point of land appears on
2103 # the correct side of the IDL, according to the date legally observed there.
2104 #
2105 # When Kiribati adopted a uniform date in 1995, thereby moving the Phoenix and
2106 # Line Islands to the west side of the IDL (or, if you prefer, moving the IDL
2107 # to the east side of the Phoenix and Line Islands), I suppose that most
2108 # mapmakers redrew the IDL following the boundary of Kiribati.  Even that line
2109 # has a rather arbitrary nature.  The straight-line boundaries between Pacific
2110 # island nations that are shown on many maps are based on an international
2111 # convention, but are not legally binding national borders.... The date is
2112 # governed by the IDL; therefore, even on the high seas, there may be some
2113 # places as late as fourteen hours later than UTC.  And, since the IDL is not
2114 # an international standard, there are some places on the high seas where the
2115 # correct date is ambiguous.

2117 # From Wikipedia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_zone> (2005-08-31):
2118 # Before 1920, all ships kept local apparent time on the high seas by setting
2119 # their clocks at night or at the morning sight so that, given the ship's
2120 # speed and direction, it would be 12 o'clock when the Sun crossed the ship's
2121 # meridian (12 o'clock = local apparent noon).  During 1917, at the
2122 # Anglo-French Conference on Time-keeping at Sea, it was recommended that all
2123 # ships, both military and civilian, should adopt hourly standard time zones
2124 # on the high seas.  Whenever a ship was within the territorial waters of any
2125 # nation it would use that nation's standard time.  The captain was permitted
2126 # to change his ship's clocks at a time of his choice following his ship's
2127 # entry into another zone time - he often chose midnight.  These zones were
2128 # adopted by all major fleets between 1920 and 1925 but not by many
2129 # independent merchant ships until World War II.
